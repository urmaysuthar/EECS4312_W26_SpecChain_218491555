# Application: [MindDoc]

- Most important difference between pipelines: [The manual pipeline produced more focused groups and personas from the start because each one was built by directly reading and grouping the reviews. The automated pipeline was much faster, but some of its early outputs were too broad, repetitive, or vague, especially in the grouping and requirement stages. The hybrid pipeline kept the speed and structure of the automated pipeline, but improved the quality by refining the themes, personas, requirements, and tests by hand.]

- Most useful pipeline: [The hybrid pipeline was the most useful overall because it gave the best balance between speed and quality. The manual pipeline gave the clearest understanding of the app reviews, but it took the most effort. The automated pipeline helped produce a full first draft quickly, but some outputs were not strong enough to keep as they were. The hybrid pipeline fixed those weak parts without forcing the whole project to be rebuilt from scratch.]

- Most surprising finding: [The most surprising finding was that the automated pipeline could produce outputs that looked complete on the surface but still had important quality problems underneath. Some requirements were grammatically correct, but they were not grounded well enough in the grouped reviews, and some test scenarios only looked reasonable because they copied those weak requirements. I also found it interesting that some of the final metric counts stayed the same between the automated and hybrid pipelines even though the hybrid version was clearly stronger in quality. That showed me that metrics alone do not tell the full story.]

- Observed weakness in the automated pipeline: [The biggest weakness in the automated pipeline was that it sometimes generated themes and requirements that sounded reasonable but were either too vague, too broad, or not fully supported by the review evidence. In earlier automated runs, some review groups overlapped too much, and later some generated requirements included ideas like hover behavior on a mobile app or extra system details that were never strongly justified by the reviews. This made it clear that automation was useful for speed and coverage, but it still needed human review before the outputs were reliable enough to treat as final.]
